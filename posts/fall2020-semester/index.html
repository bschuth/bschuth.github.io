Fall 2020 Semester

September 23, 2020

I haven’t been in college for decades, but I always feel as though I should be returning to school in September. Looking at where I am at this point in my life, what courses should I be taking?

For work, I am about to make a transition to C++, and I’m struggling with questions about the design of the platform I have to live in. In my personal life, I’ve used this unusual year to get myself into better running and biking shape than I have been in before, and don’t want to lose that. Also, I made progress in returning to my study of ancient Greek, but am currently stalled.

So let’s say three classes:

  • Up to Speed With C++, a mostly lab-based practical class where you work through examples from tutorial texts, and perhaps address some algorithmic problems.
  • Physical Education: Endurance, which is just my current run/bike schedule (I’m hoping to perhaps run the Millinocket Half-Marathon in December, or perhaps will do a long bike ride around the Columbus Day week I am taking off).
  • Intensive Ancient Greek: Working my way through Hansen and Quinn again, with the aim to quickly get to reading Plato’s Ion.

The Greek class is something of a re-take of the class I took in 1988, but this time I really hope to master it.

There’s no need for a more detailed syllabus here; but the Up to Speed With C++ needs a list of text books. I think I just need two, both of which I’m reading through the O’Reilly Learning site. I was fortunate to lock in a fairly cheap lifetime rate many years back when they were struggling so I’m not paying quite what they demand now. Compared to what I was spending back in the old days on computer books, it’s a spectacular deal:

  • C++ Cookbook, a type-along tutorial book that gives me the kind of mechnical work that makes theory and concepts turn into something usable and practical;
  • The C++ Programming Language (Fourth Edition) by the redoubtable Bjarne Stroustrup; if I can’t figure out C++ from that, I should think twice about trying it, because that really is the Bible of the language; and
  • Selections from The Art of Computer Programming by Donald Knuth. OK, this kind of a lie and/or cheat. I bought these books years ago and once or twice a year I try to read them, but they are a formidable adversary. It’s not enough that the concepts are expressed in assembly language, but they are in an imagined assembly language of Knuth’s own design (and there’s a 64-bit version being developed now). Also, I’m not proud to confess that proof by induction has never felt fluent to me; so of course he makes it the cornerstone of the introduction to the book. Oh, and the first 230 pages of the 630 page first volume are on “mathematical preliminaries.” Nevertheless I’m going to try to use it as a source book for things to play around with in C++.

I think I need some sort of schedule for C++ and Greek, to give some idea of my strategy.
